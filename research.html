Research

My traditional literary research interests cluster around the interplay between community and the individual, with repeated side nods to questions of violence. My dissertation, uncovering a range of different visions of community, noted violence as a destructive undercurrent in Ralph Hamor's quasi-familial interracial Virginia community, while John Smith's take on the same colonial community leaned on violence as a constructive element. My Master's thesis, investigating the construction of individual self in late 18th-century gothic novels by Charles Brockden Brown, found violence among a series of ruptures undermining the construction of an autonomous selfhood insulated from the pressures of transatlantic modernity. I have also pursued variegated side projects spanning from Russian Formalist literary theory to the adaptation of South African anti-Apartheid text in Soviet ballet.

Transitioning to quantitative methods, my computational text analysis practice started out with topic modeling a 30-year run of a 20th-century Canadian journal to create an interactive index of its recurring topics. Currently, I am engaged in a text-mining exploration of the Principal Navigations, a late 16th-century compilation of travel narratives assembled by Richard Hakluyt.

Dissertation Abstract

Bonds and Boundaries: Constructions of Community in Seventeenth-Century Anglo-American Colonies

Six decades of colonial writing demonstrate diverse models and practices of community ... a series of attempts, each responding to the different writer’s unique circumstances and temperaments.

This dissertation surveys a range of community visions from the early stages of British colonization of North America. While the political and social aspects of the settlements have been studied extensively, the softer matters of interpersonal ties of mutual care have only been partially addressed through affect studies. Engaging with various seventeenth-century colonial texts, I bring out diverse attempts to bind the settlers in each location into a coherent whole. After an introduction that establishes the basic terms of the discussion through John Winthrop’s “A Modell of Christian Charity,” I begin with Ralph Hamor, a minor figure from early Jamestown who articulates a unique vision of a joint Anglo-Native community. John Smith, read through a communal lens, shows a deep split between an ideal communal aspiration and an opposing tendency toward authoritarian violence. William Bradford’s Separatism shows in his persistent concern with community boundaries, which, properly attended to, reconciles the traditional declension reading with newer economic history approaches. Roger Williams takes a step further and completely dissociates spiritual communion from practical questions of earthly cohabitation, countering his frequent misapprehension as a multiculturalist liberal before his time. Anne Bradstreet, commonly recognized for her personal lyrics, uses the family and gender structures as a fundamental underpinning of political community throughout the broad reaches of her writing. The conclusion briefly revisits the case studies in formulating a counter to nationalist appropriations of early colonial community visions.

Povzner, Anton. 2021. Bonds and Boundaries: Constructions of Community in Seventeenth-Century Anglo-American Colonies. U of Notre Dame, PhD dissertation.
